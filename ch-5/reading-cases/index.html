Chapter 5 · Educational

How to Read a Case

You don’t read an opinion front to back like a story. You read it for the parts that answer your question, and you read the caption first — because the court and jurisdiction tell you whether the case is even worth your time. Here’s the anatomy, on a real opinion.

  1. 1Determine the weight before you invest the time. Start with the caption: the jurisdiction and court level tell you whether the case binds you or merely persuades.
  2. 2Read with your question as the lens. Pull out what answers it; log the rest as later questions.
  3. 3Find the dispositive facts — the ones that, if changed, would change the result.
  4. 4Confirm it’s still good law with a citator before you rely on it.
Click any part of the opinion to learn what it is and how to read it. The issue and holding are tinted in Question-Method blue — the issue is the court’s version of Step 1.

Select any highlighted section of Glik v. Cunniffe on the left and its explanation appears here — what the part is, and how a researcher reads it.

So — would you cite it? Glik is a First Circuit decision: binding on federal courts in the First Circuit, persuasive everywhere else. Deciding which cases are worth citing for a given matter — jurisdiction, court level, how on-point they are, and whether they’re still good law — is its own skill. You’ll practice it in Case Law Practice.
