Chapter 5 · Educational

How to Read a Case

You don’t read an opinion front to back like a story — and you rarely read it for just one thing. Here’s the anatomy on a real multi-issue opinion, Nash v. Tindall Corp., then two ways to work it: reading for the answer to a question you carry, and reading for class.

How to engage with an opinion — the five moves

  1. Read the caption firstCourt and jurisdiction decide whether this case can bind you — determine the weight before you invest the time.
  2. Find the issue(s)The court’s version of Step 1 of the Question Method: the questions this opinion exists to answer. One case often answers several.
  3. Pull the holding(s)The court’s answer to each issue — the parts with precedential force. Read with your question as the lens; log what answers other questions.
  4. Mine the reasoningWhat the rule turns on, and the dispositive facts — the ones that, if changed, would change the result. When your facts differ, this is where your argument lives.
  5. Check the treatmentRun the citator before you rely — a case can look perfect and no longer be good law.

Reading a Case for an Answer

So — would you cite it? Nash is a published South Carolina Court of Appeals decision: binding on South Carolina trial courts, persuasive everywhere else — including on the very question it flags as open elsewhere. Deciding which cases are worth citing for a given matter — jurisdiction, court level, how on-point, and whether still good law — is its own skill. You’ll practice it in Case Law Practice.
