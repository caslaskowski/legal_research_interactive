Trace the Bill

A statute’s words sometimes run out before your question does — and when they do, you go back to the documents the legislature left behind on the way to enactment. Those documents are not created equal. This lesson walks the process first, so you know where each document comes from; then it takes each source in depth — what it is, the weight it carries, the questions it answers, and when to reach for it.

How to use this: three parts. Walk the pipeline to see what each stage of the process leaves behind. Then open each source in the deep dive — the weight bands run from most authoritative to least. Finish inside a committee report, the one document worth knowing from the inside. Everything here is illustrative; the process is real.

The route in, and the caution out

Route: compiled legislative histories first. Before assembling documents stage by stage, check whether someone already has: the major services and government sources publish compiled legislative histories keyed to the public-law number. Start from the statute’s Public Law citation, look for a compiled history, and only build your own trail when none exists.

Caution: persuasive, never binding. Legislative history is evidence of meaning, not law. It persuades some judges and actively repels others — textualist judges may discount everything you just learned to weigh. Know your court before you build an argument on it.
