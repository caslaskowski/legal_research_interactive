How to Read a Secondary Source

A secondary source talks about the law rather than being the law. It is a map, not the territory: you use it to find and understand primary authority, and with rare exceptions you do not cite it as the law itself. Reading one well is the Question Method applied to a page — every part of the page exists to answer a certain kind of question.

How to engage with the source

  1. Match the source type to your question type. A no-clue exploratory question wants an encyclopedia; a foundational question wants a treatise; a survey question across jurisdictions wants an ALR annotation, a Restatement, or a 50-state survey. Choosing the right secondary source for the question you are holding is the first efficiency the method buys you.
  2. Navigate — do not read end to end. Use the table of contents and index to jump to the section that answers your current question.
  3. Mine the footnotes in the order your question dictates. When a passage's footnotes point to half a dozen cases, read the one that answers the question you are holding right now, and log the rest as later questions with their citations noted.
  4. Confirm against primary authority, and check currency. A secondary source can be out of date, can oversimplify, or can be wrong. Go read the primary authority it points to, and note the "current through" date.
  5. Cite primary authority, not the secondary source. With narrow exceptions, you build the argument on the authority the source led you to.

Your question is: "How do the fifty states handle a landlord's duty to provide heat?" Which secondary source does that question point to?
