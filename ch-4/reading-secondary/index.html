How to Read a Secondary Source

A secondary source talks about the law rather than being the law. It is a map, not the territory: you use it to find and understand primary authority, and with rare exceptions you do not cite it as the law itself. Below is a demonstration encyclopedia entry, rendered the way a database renders one — a navigable table of contents on the left, the document in the middle. Reading it well is the Question Method applied to a page.

How to use this: first explore the anatomy — click the table of contents, any sentence of the narrative, a footnote, the currency line, and the research references, and the Parts of the source panel explains each. Then run the Work the page exercise on the right: it hands you a question, you find the sentence that answers it, and you pull the citation from that sentence’s footnote — the move this whole appendix exists to teach.

How to engage with a secondary source — the five moves

  1. Match the source type to your question typeA no-clue exploratory question wants an encyclopedia; a foundational question wants a treatise; a survey question across jurisdictions wants an ALR annotation, a Restatement, or a 50-state survey.
  2. Navigate — do not read end to endUse the table of contents and index to jump to the section that answers your current question. That is what the sidebar is for.
  3. Find the sentence, then mine its footnoteHold your question, scan the narrative for the sentence that answers it, and follow that sentence’s footnote to the primary authority — one case now; log the rest as later questions with their citations noted.
  4. Confirm against primary authority, and check currencyA secondary source can be out of date, can oversimplify, or can be wrong. Go read what it points to, and note the “current through” date.
  5. Cite primary authority, not the secondary sourceWith narrow exceptions, you build the argument on the authority the source led you to.
