Ride the Line

Think of your research questions as a subway map. Each chain of questions is a colored line; each question–answer pair is a station; the end of the line is that thread’s closure. The danger is the interchange: mid-ride, a source answers a question from a different line — and the tempting exit is exactly where research derails into cognitive overload. The discipline: note the transfer, stay on your line to the end, then teleport to the next line. This module lets you feel both choices.

How to use this: ride the map below station by station. At the interchange you’ll face the real choice — pick either one; the wrong exit teaches as much as the right one. Watch the Research Log on the right: a noted transfer becomes a ticket, and tickets get spent later.
Demonstration — fictional cases & jurisdiction

Before boarding

Your client’s apartment has had almost no heat for three weeks. Two threads of questions have formed: Line 1 — does the landlord owe a duty, and what does it require? and Line 2 — what can the tenant do about it?

Board Line 1. You’ll ride it station by station — one question, one answer, next question — all the way to the end.

Research Log — Notes

    Empty for now. Transfers you note will appear here as tickets.

    The subway rules

    What you just practiced, as rules you can carry.

    Rule 1Ride your line to the endA thread is closed when its last question is answered — not when a shinier question appears.
    Rule 2Interchanges are gifts — if you log themA source that answers another line’s question costs you ten seconds to note and saves you a station later.
    Rule 3Never chase mid-lineExiting early leaves open questions behind you and opens new ones ahead — that pile of half-ridden lines is what cognitive overload is.
    Rule 4Teleport only at a terminusWhen a line closes, jump to the next line’s first station and ride it fully — even where it crosses old track.
    Rule 5The log is your transfer ticketThe Notes field is external memory. What’s written down doesn’t have to be held in your head.

    Two quick checks

    Mid-chain on question 1.b, a case in your results squarely answers question 2.c from a different thread. The method’s move?

    Why insist on riding a line all the way to its end before switching?
