Chapter 3 · Orientation

Reverse-Engineering the Question

Every well-built paragraph of legal analysis is the fossil record of a research process. Below is one paragraph from a real opinion, Oakland Police & Fire Ret. Sys. v. Brown, 861 F.3d 644 (7th Cir. 2017) (parallel citations removed for brevity). First, take it apart into the questions that built it. Then watch the research that produces it, mapped step by step.

The paragraph, quoted with its citations — the citations are the evidence. Watch how the first three propositions each rest on one authority, while the last carries a string of them.

In the vast majority of legal malpractice cases, the plaintiff was a client of the defendant-attorney, and the direct attorney-client relationship establishes the attorney’s professional duties to the client. Pelham v. Griesheimer, 92 Ill. 2d 13, 440 N.E.2d 96, 99 (Ill. 1982). There is a small corner of legal malpractice law, however, in which Illinois recognizes that attorneys owe professional duties to persons who are not their clients. In Pelham, the Illinois Supreme Court held that an attorney may owe a duty to a third party when “the primary purpose and intent of the attorney-client relationship itself was to benefit or influence the third party.” Id. at 100. Examples of third parties to whom attorneys owe a duty include third-party beneficiaries of wills, third-party beneficiaries of wrongful death actions, and third-party recipients of formal opinion letters. See In re Estate of Powell, 382 Ill.Dec. 14; Geaslen v. Berkson, Gorov & Levin, Ltd. (Geaslen II), 184 Ill.Dec. 385 (1993); McLane v. Russell, 137 Ill.Dec. 554 (1989).

Notice the last one. The first proposition rests on Pelham alone; the test carries a pinpoint Id.; but the examples sentence ends in a string citation — three cases for one proposition. That string is the proof: no single case supplies the list, so the writer surveyed several. A string cite is the visible residue of a survey question.

Let’s map their research

Same colors everywhere: each question’s hue ties the research log to the case chart to the annotated outline.

Research Log

  • 1Q1 opened (focusing): Who does an attorney owe professional duties to? → treatise on legal malpractice → leads to Pelham v. Griesheimer (Ill. 1982). Answered.
  • 2Q2 logged while reading Pelham: the case also addresses whether a duty can ever run to a non-client. Added to the chart as a new column.
  • 3Q3 logged while reading Pelham: it also states the test for that exception. Added as a third column.
  • 4Q2 answered from Pelham: yes — a narrow exception exists. Cell filled; outline updated.
  • 5Q3 answered from Pelham, Id. at 100: the primary-purpose test. Cell filled; quotation captured for the outline.
  • 6Q4 opened (survey): Pelham states the test but gives no examples of relationships that satisfy it. New question: what types of relationships meet the primary-purpose test? This needs a survey across cases.
  • 7Q4 surveyed: McLane (wills), In re Estate of Powell (wrongful death), Geaslen II (opinion letters). All three cite Pelham for the rule, the exception, and the test — so Q1–Q3 need no new rows of answers; Pelham is already a sufficiently authoritative source for those premises. Only the Q4 cells get filled.
  • 8Checkpoint: survey synthesized into three one-line propositions; outline completed; ready to draft.

Annotated Outline

A. In the vast majority of legal malpractice cases, the plaintiff was a client of the defendant-attorney, and the direct attorney-client relationship establishes the attorney’s professional duties to the client. Pelham v. Griesheimer, 92 Ill. 2d 13, 440 N.E.2d 96, 99 (Ill. 1982).

B. Pelham did note an exception where an attorney may owe a duty to a third party when “the primary purpose and intent of the attorney-client relationship itself was to benefit or influence the third party.” Id. at 100.

C. Examples of third parties to whom attorneys owe a duty include:

  • Third-party beneficiaries of wills. McLane v. Russell, 137 Ill.Dec. 554 (1989)
  • Third-party beneficiaries of wrongful death actions. In re Estate of Powell, 382 Ill.Dec. 14 (2014)
  • Third-party recipients of formal opinion letters. Geaslen v. Berkson, Gorov & Levin, Ltd. (Geaslen II), 184 Ill.Dec. 385 (1993)
