The ADA Canvas

Module pagesOverview
Learning resourcesKnowledge checks

What this module teaches: a single research question rarely lives in a single source. The canvas starts with exactly one card — the general rule — and every other card must be earned by following a reference, the way research actually works. As you click through cross-references and defined terms, the statute’s definitions, the regulation, and two lines of cases appear and wire themselves together — and one underlined phrase turns out to be the exact spot where federal courts split.

How to use this: read the card, then click its teal links to reveal the sources it points to, and its red terms to weigh competing definitions. Scroll the canvas freely (arrow keys work; zoom bottom-left). When cards multiply, the Rearrange button tidies the visible ones into columns so the connections read clearly. Every card is real law: statutes and regulation verbatim, holdings summarized, and opinion quotations verified — watch for where the cases quote the very statute you started on.
Real law — verified 2026-07-10
Statute42 U.S.C. § 12182(a) — General rule

No individual shall be discriminated against on the basis of disability in the full and equal enjoyment of the goods, services, facilities, privileges, advantages, or accommodations of any by any person who owns, leases (or leases to), or operates a place of public accommodation.

Three threads leave this card. The key phrase is defined in — and again, differently, in . And the rule’s teeth live in .

Statute · definitions42 U.S.C. § 12181(7) — Public accommodation

The following private entities are considered public accommodations for purposes of this subchapter, if the operations of such entities affect commerce—

(A)–(D) omitted here · lodging, food, entertainment, gathering

(E) a bakery, grocery store, clothing store, hardware store, shopping center, or other sales or rental establishment;

(F) a laundromat, dry-cleaner, bank, barber shop, beauty shop, travel service, shoe repair service, funeral parlor, gas station, office of an accountant or lawyer, pharmacy, insurance office, professional office of a health care provider, hospital, or other service establishment;

(G)–(K) omitted here · transit, display, recreation, education, social service

(L) a gymnasium, health spa, bowling alley, golf course, or other place of exercise or recreation.

Twelve categories of entities — grocery stores and golf courses, but also “travel service” and “insurance office.” Do those need a building? .

Statute§ 12182(b)(2)(A)(ii) — Reasonable modifications

[Discrimination includes] a failure to make reasonable modifications in policies, practices, or procedures, when such modifications are necessary to afford such goods, services, facilities, privileges, advantages, or accommodations to individuals with disabilities, unless the entity can demonstrate that making such modifications would the nature of such goods, services, facilities, privileges, advantages, or accommodations;

The duty — and its limit. In 2000, two circuits applied it to the same question and split: and .

Regulation28 C.F.R. § 36.104 — Definitions

Place of public accommodation means a operated by a private entity whose operations affect commerce and fall within at least one of the following categories…

Public accommodation means a private entity that owns, leases (or leases to), or operates a place of public accommodation.

The agency added a word the statute never used: facility — and every noun in its definition is physical. .

Case · 7th Cir.Olinger v. U.S. Golf Ass’n, 205 F.3d 1001 (2000)
Holding, summarizedA golfer with a mobility disability could not require the U.S. Open to allow a cart: the Seventh Circuit concluded that walking was part of the competition, so waiving it would fundamentally alter the nature of the event.

One side of a live circuit split. .

Case · 9th Cir.Martin v. PGA Tour, 204 F.3d 994 (2000)
Holding, summarizedThe Ninth Circuit went the other way: PGA tournament golf courses are places of public accommodation even inside the ropes, and letting Casey Martin ride did not fundamentally alter tournament golf.

Same statute, same year, opposite answers — a square split. .

Case · Supreme CourtPGA Tour, Inc. v. Martin, 532 U.S. 661 (2001)
Holding, summarized — split resolvedThe Court affirmed the Ninth Circuit: Title III reaches the Tour’s competitions, and a cart is a reasonable modification — walking is not fundamental to the game.
From the opinion — quoting the statute“The events occur on ‘golf course[s],’ a type of place specifically identified by the Act as a public accommodation. § 12181(7)(L).” … “[T]he walking rule is at best peripheral to the nature of petitioner’s athletic events, and thus it might be waived in individual cases without working a fundamental alteration.”

A resolved split on the shelf: the 2000 circuit opinions still exist, but the citator points above them. Note the Court reading § 12181(7)(L) — the very card your first click revealed.

Case · 1st Cir.Carparts Distrib. Ctr. v. Auto. Wholesaler’s Ass’n, 37 F.3d 12 (1994)
Holding, summarizedReading § 12181(7)’s categories — “travel service,” “insurance office” — the First Circuit held public accommodations are not limited to physical structures.
From the opinion — reading § 12181(7)(F)“The plain meaning of the terms do not require ‘public accommodations’ to have physical structures for persons to enter.” … “It would be irrational to conclude that persons who enter an office to purchase services are protected by the ADA, but persons who purchase the same services over the telephone or by mail are not. Congress could not have intended such an absurd result.”

The broad reading, staked out before the commercial web existed. .

Case · 9th Cir.Weyer v. Twentieth Century Fox, 198 F.3d 1104 (2000)
Holding, summarizedThe categories in § 12181(7) are actual, physical places — so a covered good or service needs a connection to one.
The test, as later quoted by RoblesFor the ADA to apply there must be “some connection between the good or service complained of and an actual physical place.”

The narrow reading — and the seed of the “nexus” requirement. .

Case · 9th Cir.Robles v. Domino’s Pizza, 913 F.3d 898 (2019)
Holding, summarizedThe nexus rule, operational: the ADA covers Domino’s website and app because they connect customers to the goods and services of its physical restaurants.
From the opinion — citing the statute“The alleged inaccessibility of Domino’s website and app impedes access to the goods and services of its physical pizza franchises—which are places of public accommodation. See 42 U.S.C. § 12181(7)(B) (listing a restaurant as a covered ‘public accommodation’).”

Screen-reader inaccessibility can violate Title III when the site serves a physical place. .

Case · 11th Cir.Gil v. Winn-Dixie Stores, 993 F.3d 1266 (2021)
Holding, summarizedThe panel held websites are not places of public accommodation: Title III’s listed categories are all physical.
From the panel opinion — reading § 12181(7)“All of these listed types of locations are tangible, physical places. No intangible places or spaces, such as websites, are listed. Thus, we conclude that, pursuant to the plain language of Title III of the ADA, public accommodations are limited to actual, physical places.”
⚠ Vacated. On rehearing, the panel vacated this opinion as moot — the injunction had expired mid-appeal. Gil v. Winn-Dixie Stores, 21 F.4th 775 (11th Cir. Dec. 28, 2021). A vacated opinion is no longer precedent: the Eleventh Circuit currently has no published answer, and a researcher who cites the panel opinion without running a citator cites a ghost.

The open split, today: the Ninth Circuit’s nexus rule stands; other circuits diverge or haven’t answered; the Supreme Court has not resolved it. This cluster is what an outstanding split looks like — and why validation is not optional.
