Duties Behind the Research

Research quality is not just craft — it is professional obligation. Four duties from the Model Rules stand behind every research decision you will make, and each one has a specific moment in the research cycle where it bites. Learn the four, then match the moments.

How to use this: read the four duty cards — especially the “where it bites” line on each — then work the matching exercise below. The practice modules in this chapter put these duties under deadline pressure.

The four duties

Model Rule 1.1

Competence

A lawyer must provide competent representation — the legal knowledge, skill, thoroughness, and preparation the matter requires. Comment 8 extends it to technology: keeping abreast of the benefits and risks of the tools of practice.

Where it bites in research: thoroughness is a research duty. Skipping the citator, stopping before the question is answered, or using an AI tool without understanding how it fails are all competence problems before they are anything else.

Model Rule 3.3

Candor to the tribunal

A lawyer may not knowingly make a false statement of law to a court, and must disclose controlling authority directly adverse to the client’s position if opposing counsel has not.

Where it bites in research: twice. A citation you never verified can make you the one presenting false authority — and the adverse case your research surfaces is not optional to deal with. Finding it created the duty.

Model Rule 1.6

Confidentiality

A lawyer must not reveal information relating to the representation, and must make reasonable efforts to prevent unauthorized disclosure.

Where it bites in research: the tools. Client facts typed into a free public AI tool, a search history on a shared account, a docket request that telegraphs a deal — research inputs can be disclosures.

Model Rules 5.1 & 5.3

Supervision

Lawyers with supervisory authority must make reasonable efforts to ensure that subordinate lawyers’ and nonlawyers’ work conforms to professional obligations.

Where it bites in research: the sign-off. A supervisor who files a junior’s unverified memo owns the failure — and “the associate did the research” (or “the AI did”) has never been a defense.
